Solar System Model: Create a Model of the Solar System Using Household Objects

By Karthik Kumar D K | Published: Jul 7, 2026 10:51 AM IST
By Karthik Kumar D K , 7 July 2026
Model of the Solar System Using Household Objects

A Fun and Easy DIY Science Project for Kids

Have you ever looked up at the night sky and wondered how the planets travel around the Sun? The solar system is one of the most exciting topics children learn in school because it introduces them to the amazing world beyond Earth. While reading books and watching videos can help, nothing makes learning more memorable than building a solar system model with your own hands.

The best part is that you don't need expensive craft kits or special science equipment. Many of the materials needed can already be found around your home. By using simple household objects like paper, old newspapers, bottle caps, cardboard, paint, string, and glue, children can create a colorful model of the solar system while learning about each planet in a fun and interactive way.

This DIY project is perfect for school science fairs, classroom activities, homeschooling, family weekends, and rainy-day learning. It encourages creativity, improves problem-solving skills, and helps children understand the order of the planets through hands-on experience.

In this guide, you'll learn everything you need to know to create an easy solar system model using household objects, along with fun facts, educational tips, and creative ideas to make your project stand out.

What Is the Solar System?

The solar system is made up of the Sun and everything that travels around it because of its powerful gravity. This includes eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and millions of smaller space objects.

The Sun sits at the center of the solar system and provides light and heat to all the planets. Each planet moves around the Sun in its own path, called an orbit.

The eight planets, in order from the Sun, are:

  1. Mercury
  2. Venus
  3. Earth
  4. Mars
  5. Jupiter
  6. Saturn
  7. Uranus
  8. Neptune

Remembering the order of the planets becomes much easier when children build the model themselves.

Why Build a Solar System Model?

Making a model is much more than an art activity. It helps children understand scientific concepts in a practical way.

Children learn:

  • The order of the planets
  • Relative planet sizes
  • Basic astronomy concepts
  • Creativity and design
  • Fine motor skills
  • Observation skills
  • Patience and teamwork

Teachers often recommend building models because children remember information better when they create something themselves.

Household Items You'll Need

One of the best things about this project is that most materials are already available at home.

You can use:

  • Cardboard or a shoe box
  • Old newspapers
  • Balloons
  • Aluminum foil
  • Cotton balls
  • Bottle caps
  • Paper plates
  • Colored paper
  • Acrylic or poster paints
  • Paint brushes
  • Glue
  • Scissors
  • Black chart paper
  • Thread or string
  • Toothpicks
  • Wooden skewers
  • Markers

Don't worry if you don't have every item. Feel free to substitute with whatever is available around your house.

Step 1: Prepare the Base

Start by selecting a sturdy base.

A large piece of cardboard, a shoe box lid, or foam board works perfectly.

Paint the surface black or dark blue to represent outer space.

Once the paint dries, add tiny white dots using a paintbrush or white marker to create stars.

You can even sprinkle a little glitter to make your galaxy sparkle.

Step 2: Make the Sun

The Sun should be the largest object in your model.

There are several easy ways to create it:

  • Crumple newspaper into a ball and cover it with yellow paper.
  • Use a balloon covered with paper mache.
  • Paint a foam ball bright yellow and orange.
  • Cover a paper ball with gold-colored paper.

Remember that the Sun is a giant star, not a planet.

Place it at one side or in the center, depending on your project design.

Step 3: Create the Planets

Now comes the most exciting part.

Create each planet using paper balls, clay, foam balls, or rolled newspaper.

Paint them using their natural colors.

PlanetNatural Colors
MercurySmall and gray.
VenusYellowish-white with thick clouds.
Earth

Blue, green, and white.

Mars

Reddish-orange.

Jupiter

The biggest planet with brown and white stripes.

Saturn

Light yellow with beautiful rings.

Uranus

Light blue.

Neptune

Deep blue.

Try making each planet a different size to show that some planets are much larger than others.

Step 4: Add Saturn's Rings

Saturn is famous for its spectacular rings.

Cut a ring shape from cardboard or thick paper.

Paint it light brown or gray.

Glue it around Saturn.

This simple step makes your model look much more realistic.

Step 5: Arrange the Planets

Now arrange the planets in the correct order.

Starting from the Sun:

Mercury → Venus → Earth → Mars → Jupiter → Saturn → Uranus → Neptune

Leave some space between each planet to represent their orbits.

You can use string or paint circles around the Sun to show the orbital paths.

Step 6: Label Everything

Use small paper flags or labels to write the name of each planet.

This helps children remember the planets while presenting the project in school.

You may also include:

  • The Sun
  • Asteroid Belt
  • Moon
  • Saturn's Rings

Adding labels makes the project educational as well as attractive.

Fun Facts About the Planets

Here are some exciting facts children love learning

PlanetFun Facts
Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and has no atmosphere to protect it from extreme temperatures.

Venus

Venus is the hottest planet in the solar system, even hotter than Mercury.

Earth

Earth is the only known planet with life.

Mars

Mars is often called the Red Planet because of its reddish soil.

Jupiter

Jupiter is so large that more than 1,300 Earths could fit inside it.

Saturn

Saturn has thousands of beautiful rings made mostly of ice and rock.

Uranus

Unlike most planets, Uranus rotates on its side.

Neptune

Neptune has the fastest winds in the solar system.

Make Your Model More Creative

Want your project to stand out?

Try adding:

  • Glow-in-the-dark stars
  • LED lights behind the Sun
  • Cotton clouds
  • Small astronaut figures
  • Toy rockets
  • Alien characters
  • A cardboard galaxy background

These creative additions make the model even more exciting for younger children.

Tips for Parents and Teachers

This activity becomes even more meaningful when adults participate.

Instead of building the project for children, encourage them to make decisions independently.

Ask questions like:

  • Which planet is the biggest?
  • Which planet is closest to the Sun?
  • Why does Earth have life?
  • Which planet has rings?

These conversations help children understand science while having fun.

Safety Tips

Always keep safety in mind during craft activities.

  • Use child-safe scissors.
  • Adult supervision is recommended.
  • Keep glue away from eyes.
  • Wash hands after painting.
  • Use non-toxic craft materials.

Safety allows children to enjoy learning without unnecessary risks.

Benefits of This DIY Science Project

Building a solar system model develops many important skills.

Children improve:

  • Creativity
  • Scientific thinking
  • Concentration
  • Hand-eye coordination
  • Presentation skills
  • Memory
  • Confidence

Projects like these make science enjoyable rather than intimidating.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Can preschool children make this project?

Yes. Younger children can paint the planets while adults help with cutting and assembly.

2. How long does the project take?

Most families complete it in two to four hours.

3. Can recycled materials be used?

Absolutely. Recycling newspapers, cardboard, bottle caps, and packaging materials makes this project environmentally friendly.

4. Is this suitable for school exhibitions?

Yes. This is one of the most popular science fair projects for primary school students because it is colorful, educational, and easy to explain.

Conclusion

Creating a solar system model using household objects is one of the most enjoyable ways for children to explore space while developing creativity and scientific curiosity. With just a few everyday materials, kids can transform cardboard, paper, paint, and recycled items into a miniature version of our amazing solar system.

As they build each planet, arrange them in order, and decorate their project, children naturally learn about astronomy in a fun, hands-on way. Whether the model is for a classroom assignment, a science exhibition, or simply an exciting weekend activity, it encourages learning through imagination and discovery.

Remember, the goal isn't to create a perfect model—it's to inspire young minds to ask questions, explore new ideas, and develop a lifelong love for science. So gather your household supplies, invite the whole family to join in, and start building your own colorful solar system today. Every great scientist begins with curiosity, and this simple DIY project could be the first step toward a child's exciting journey into the wonders of space.
